Onam - Kerala's Harvest Festival

Onam is Kerala's most significant cultural festival, celebrated with vibrant enthusiasm across the state. It commemorates the legendary return of King Mahabali and falls in the Malayalam month of Chingam (Aug-Sep). The ten-day festival beautifully blends tradition, community spirit, and artistic expression.


  • Pookalams: Intricate floral carpets laid at entrances to welcome King Mahabali.
  • Onasadya: A grand vegetarian feast served on banana leaves with over 20 dishes.
  • Vallam Kali: Snake boat races on the backwaters that thrill spectators and reflect unity.
  • Pulikali: Tiger dance where men paint themselves as tigers and perform to rhythmic beats.
  • Traditional Games: Talappanthukali (ball game), Vadamvali (tug of war), and more promote camaraderie.
  • Art & Culture: Folk dances, classical music, and local plays enhance the festive ambiance.
  • Community Bonding: Onam is celebrated across religions, promoting inclusiveness and harmony.

Onam bridges myth and modernity, faith and fun. It is a tribute to justice, prosperity, and the golden reign of Mahabali, where equality and joy prevailed.

Vishu - Malayalam New Year

Vishu signifies the Malayalam New Year and is celebrated with joy, hope, and spiritual reflection. It falls in April and marks the sun's entry into the zodiac sign Medam, symbolizing fresh beginnings.


  • Vishukkani: An arrangement of auspicious items like rice, golden flowers (Konna), fruits, mirror, and scriptures placed before Lord Krishna.
  • First Sight: Devotees wake at dawn and view the Vishukkani as the first sight of the year for blessings.
  • Vishukkaineetam: Elders gift money to children and dependents as a symbol of prosperity and good will.
  • Fireworks: Children light crackers, and joy spreads across households and temples alike.
  • Feast: A special Vishu Sadhya (meal) is prepared and shared with family and friends.
  • Temple Rituals: Special poojas and prayers are held across Kerala with festive decor and lamps.
  • Seasonal Symbolism: Vishu coincides with the harvest of pulses and fruits, reinforcing abundance and renewal.
  • Traditional Attire: People dress in new clothes, and yellow is prominently worn to reflect prosperity and energy.

Vishu blends optimism with tradition, honoring light, abundance, and the cyclical beauty of nature and life. The festival's core is gratitude—thankfulness for the past and hope for the coming year. Its rituals unite generations in shared joy, making Vishu not only a New Year but a vibrant celebration of harmony, culture, and beginnings. In every home, the golden hues of Vishukkani reflect dreams of happiness, resilience, and good fortune. Vishu continues to shine as a cherished beacon of Malayalam heritage and unity.

Thrissur Pooram - The Grand Temple Festival

Thrissur Pooram is Kerala's most iconic temple festival, showcasing the pinnacle of pageantry, sound, and spirit. Held at Vadakkunnathan Temple in Thrissur during April or May, it gathers lakhs in celebration.


  • Elephant Procession: Dozens of decorated elephants represent competing temples, Paramekkavu and Thiruvambady.
  • Kudamattam: A breathtaking exchange of colorful parasols atop elephants, done to rhythmic beats.
  • Melams: Percussion ensembles like Elanjithara Melam create a thunderous, spiritual soundscape.
  • Fireworks: A dazzling night sky show that lasts for hours and is unmatched in scale.
  • Unity: Though competitive, the event promotes communal harmony and pride in Kerala’s traditions.
  • Cultural Extravaganza: Classical music, traditional attire, and festive food bring everyone together.

More than a ritual, Thrissur Pooram is a spectacle—deeply spiritual, thrilling, and unforgettable.

Attukal Pongala - Sacred Offering by Women

Attukal Pongala is one of the world's largest religious gatherings led entirely by women. It takes place in Thiruvananthapuram in February-March, near the Attukal Bhagavathy Temple.


  • Pongala Offering: Women cook a sweet rice dish (pongala) using jaggery, banana, and coconut over temporary hearths.
  • Main Ritual: The temple priest lights the main hearth; this sacred flame is then shared and passed among devotees.
  • Citywide Participation: Streets, courtyards, and public spaces turn into sanctified grounds filled with offerings.
  • Goddess Kannaki: The goddess symbolizes feminine strength, justice, and resilience.
  • Spiritual Power: The act of devotion is simple but emotionally powerful and deeply moving.
  • Collective Energy: The atmosphere is serene, disciplined, and infused with compassion and community spirit.
  • UN Recognition: The festival has gained attention for its scale and significance in representing female-led spirituality.
  • Eco Practices: Many devotees use sustainable materials like clay pots and banana leaves to minimize waste.

Pongala embodies unity, simplicity, and the sacred feminine—a celebration of devotion beyond barriers. As women gather in peaceful solidarity, they transform urban spaces into spiritual sanctuaries filled with smoke, songs, and sacred intent. The festival is not only a devotional act but also a statement of collective strength, nurturing the spiritual bond among millions of women each year.

Theyyam - Ritual Dance of the Gods

Theyyam is a sacred ritual dance from North Kerala where performers transform into deities. Rooted in ancient tribal traditions, it is mainly performed in Kannur and Kasaragod districts.


  • Divine Possession: Performers embody gods through dance, rituals, and trance-like states.

  • Costumes & Face Art: Theyyam features striking makeup, towering headgear, and ornate garments.

  • Musical Ritual: The rhythm of chenda, thudi, and traditional chants drives the performance.

  • Sacred Spaces: Takes place in village temples and open-air shrines called Kavu (sacred groves).

  • Myth & Legend: Each Theyyam narrates a story of heroism, justice, or mythology from local lore.

Theyyam is not just an art form—it is a living connection between community, cosmos, and culture.

Kerala Literature Festival

The Kerala Literature Festival (KLF) is one of India's leading literary events, held annually in January on Kozhikode Beach. It brings together authors, thinkers, and artists from across the world in a vibrant exchange of ideas.

  • Location: Kozhikode Beach, Kerala
  • Guests: International and Indian authors, poets, artists, and academics
  • Activities: Panel discussions, readings, workshops, performances, and debates
  • Audience: Students, writers, readers, tourists, and general public
  • Theme: Literature meets environment, politics, art, and technology
  • Atmosphere: Open, inclusive, and intellectually stimulating by the sea

With its beachside setting and global lineup, KLF offers a uniquely relaxed yet deeply engaging literary experience. It is more than a festival—it is a cultural movement celebrating the power of words, freedom of thought, and the literary soul of Kerala. Each session is a conversation, each poem a performance, and each evening a celebration of ideas that shape our world.

International Film Festival of Kerala (IFFK)

The International Film Festival of Kerala (IFFK) is a prestigious cinematic event that showcases outstanding films from around the world. Held in December in Thiruvananthapuram, it is a cornerstone of Kerala's vibrant film culture.


  • Location: Multiple theaters in Thiruvananthapuram
  • Categories: World Cinema, Malayalam Cinema, LGBTQ+ Films, Documentaries, Debut Directors
  • Guests: Filmmakers, critics, journalists, students, and cinephiles from India and abroad
  • Activities: Screenings, Q&A sessions, masterclasses, red carpet events
  • Organizers: Kerala State Chalachitra Academy

IFFK fosters cinematic excellence and cultural dialogue in a democratic and accessible format. It is a platform for bold storytelling, thought-provoking narratives, and artistic brilliance from both emerging and established filmmakers. With packed auditoriums, post-screening discussions, and inclusive programming, IFFK is not just a festival—it's a movement that celebrates freedom of expression through film. The festival highlights social justice, human rights, and environmental themes through powerful visual storytelling. Films from over 70 countries are screened, and special retrospectives honor legendary directors and movements. IFFK also nurtures local talent, offering a launchpad for Kerala’s independent cinema on the global stage.

Nehru Trophy Boat Race

The Nehru Trophy Boat Race is Kerala's most thrilling aquatic spectacle, held every August on the serene waters of Punnamada Lake in Alappuzha. It celebrates tradition, teamwork, and the spirit of competition.


  • Location: Punnamada Lake, Alappuzha
  • Boats: Chundan Vallams (snake boats) rowed by over 100 oarsmen each
  • Highlights: Synchronized rowing, vanchipattu (boat songs), cheering crowds, decorated boats
  • Origin: Named after India's first Prime Minister, Jawaharlal Nehru
  • Viewership: Tens of thousands of spectators, including dignitaries and tourists

The boat race is a high-energy visual treat that pulses with tradition and unity. It's not just a competition—it is a cultural celebration that turns the backwaters of Kerala into a roaring river of pride and passion. The rhythmic strokes, chants, and community spirit make the Nehru Trophy Boat Race a must-see event in God's Own Country.
